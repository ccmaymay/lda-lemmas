Instructions

In this task, a sentence with a yellow-highlighted word is presented, followed by questions about that word. We call these words predicates: they refer to some kind of situation (an event, or a state). We are interested in how likely the situation actually happened (or will happen), and whether the situation happened (or will happen) as according to the author themselves, or if the author is instead reporting a situation described by a secondary source. If you think a situation happened (or will happen) then you will be asked for its likely duration, such seconds, or hours, or years. The very first question of the task is whether you believe the highlighted word refers to a situation at all: sometimes our automatic processing makes mistakes, and we want your help in filtering out those errors. If you have a hard time making sense of the rest of the questions for the highlighted word, then likely there was an error in creating the HIT: in those cases, please change the first option such that it reports the error, and ignore the rest of the questions for that example. We have provided a variety of examples and further discussion, broken into categories: click any of the links to hide or reveal them for clarification.

Happened

The following examples illustrate situations (events and states), and are paired with discussion on when we would say they do or do not happen.

Sue hugged Jack.

We would say that according to the author, hugged refers to a situation that did happen. As a simple test that you can use for many examples, try saying something to yourself like: "... and because of the hug, something else happened (or will happen)". If you can imagine a sentence such that as that following the example sentence provided, then that is good evidence that the situation did really happen (or will happen).

Sue wanted to hug Jack.

Here we would say that the wanting did happen. For example, we can imagine saying: "Because she wanted to hug Jack, she walked over and gave him a hug."

Sue wanted to hug Jack.

While the wanting did happen, the hug we are uncertain about: there is not enough information to say that it did or did not happen, we are unsure.

Sue will hug Jack.

According to the author a hug is very likely to happen in the future.

Sue may hug Jack.

A hug is possible, in the future.

Sue did not hug Jack.

The author is stating that the hug did not happen.

Sue probably did not want to hug Jack.

The author is unsure about whether Sue wanted to perform a hug: we might think that the sentence does or does not imply that the hugging took place. In these cases use your best judgement: if you think that the sentence implies that an event probably took place, then go ahead and say that.

I wish that Sue would hug Jack.

The situation did not happen, it is being described as a desire, not as something that actually may have happened.

Mary wishes that Sue would hug Jack.

Again, the situation did not happen.


Now we will gives examples of states, which are things that "happen" over the course of time, such being happy, or being tall.

Sue is happy.

According to the author, Sue being happy is something that is happening currently.

Sue was happy.

Sue being happy is something that happened in the past.

Generic

In langauge there is a special category of situation called generic descriptions: we will explain these here in order that you can recognize them and then say that they did not happen, for purposes of this task. We are concerned with finding just the specific, concrete situations that are being described in text, not generic descriptions.

Lions roar.

This is an example of a generic sentence: it does not refer to a specific set of lions, rather, the author is making a general claim about the world. Since the author is not describing a specific instance of roaring, then we would say the situation did not happen, and that it is generic.

Everyone knows that babies cry.

Again, there is no specific event being described as happening, it is generic.

Acme never cheats customers.

No event happened, and it is again a generic description.

Acme usually cheats customers.

No one specific event is being described, this is also a generic description (these sort of generic sentences are sometimes called habitual statements).

John likes to smoke fine cigars.

This is another example of a habitual statement: no specific event is being described, and it is generic.

//
John should excercise.
//

This is another example of a habitual statement: no specific event is being described, and it is generic.

The lions roared at the zebras.

These are specific lions roaring at a specific time and place: it is a situation that actually happened, and it is not generic.

The lions roared at the zebras on Monday and Tuesday.

These are specific lions roaring: it is a complex situation involving multiple times, but it is not generic, the roaring in this case is something that actually happened. This is different from the example of Acme usually cheating customers: in that case the author was not describing any particular situation, but was instead making a statement about a general tendency.

Secondary

We are concerned with whether the author is the one reporting on something directly, as compared to describing someone else's statements or beliefs. If something is described as if it is happening or has happened, but that the author is ascribing this to someone else, then we call this a secondary report. If something is coming from a secondary source, please judge the likelihood of something happening from the perspective of that secondary source.


Sue said that Jack was happy.

Is is not clear whether the author thinks Jack being happy actually happened: they are reporting on a statement made by Sue. You would say that the event happened, but then also check the optional box for "secondary". You might recognize this style of writing as being common in newspaper articles: reporters regularly do not state things directly, but phrase them in ways such as:

"The Martians took my husband!", said Mrs. Smith.

Here, even if it seems unlikely the author believes that the abduction happened, we would want you to judge the likelihood based on what is said of Mrs. Smith: she seems certain that the event happened.


Sue thinks that Jack is happy, but I do not.

Again, this is a secondary report of something happening, and from the perspective of that secondary source, it is likely that it happened.


Sue does not believe that Jack is happy, but I do.

Conversely, it is unlikely the situation happened, from the perspective of the secondary source.

Duration

Examples regarding the duration of a situation.


Sue remodeled her kitchen.

The remodeling happened, and it could have taken days, or more likely took weeks, or months. Use your best judgement: we would say weeks, but perhaps in your experience you would say months, and that would be OK too. Clearly the remodeling did not take seconds, nor centuries.


Sue smiled at Jack.

This likely took seconds.


Sue sneezed.

We usually think of activities like sneezes happening near-instantly, rather than "seconds".


The bomb exploded.

Similarly, explosions are things we tend to think of as events that happen in an instant.

Sue danced with Jack all evening.

This took hours.


Sue is very tall.

Being tall is not likely to change through most of a person's life, so we would say this happened over decades.


Russia is one of the largest countries in the world.

This happens over the course of centuries.


Three is prime.

This is a mathematical truth, it happens forever: we would pick the largest value available (centuries).
